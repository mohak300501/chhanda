Meter (Chhand - छन्द in Sanskrit)

A meter is a rhythmic arrangement of syllables in a verse. A specific combination of stressed and unstressed syllables essentially produces a meter. There are over a thousand different meters in Sanskrit!
Sanskrit is a phonetic language, that is, it's written and spoken forms are equivalent. The vowels are syllables in themselves but can be added to consonants or a group of consonants to create another syllable.
Hence, a syllable is associated with one vowel and zero or more consonant sounds.

Stressed and Unstressed Syllables in Sanskrit

There are two types of vowels in Sanskrit - long and short, and thus long and short syllables. In other words, the two kinds of syllables differ in the times taken to pronounce them. But if a consonant follows a
vowel, the syllable becomes long, regardless of the vowel's being short or long.
Long vowels - अ, इ, उ, ऋ
Short vowels - आ, ई, ऊ, ॠ, ए, ऐ, ओ, औ, अं, अः
E.g., भानु has two syllables - भा (भ्+आ: long) and नु (न्+उ: short); while देवस्य has three syllables - दे (द्+ए: long), वस् (व्+अ+स्: long), and य (य्+अ: short).

Meter Inspection

A short syllable is represented by ।.
A long syllable is represented by ऽ.
An example shloka is inspected as follows -


  कलत्रं च पुत्रं च मित्रादि सर्वम्
  । ऽऽ । ऽ ऽ । ऽ ऽ । ऽ ऽ
  मनःशान्तये प्राज्य मुत्सृज्य राज्यम् |
  । ऽ ऽ। ऽ ऽ ।  ऽ ऽ।  ऽ ऽ
  अपूर्वा त्वया दर्शिता त्यागवृत्तिः
  । ऽ ऽ । ऽ ऽ।  ऽ ऽ। ऽऽ
  नमस्ते महाभाग बुद्ध त्रिवारम् ||
  । ऽऽ  ।ऽ ऽ।  ऽ ऽ। ऽ ऽ

The arrangement of short and long syllables represents the movement of a snake (।ऽऽ), and thus this meter is named Bhujaṅgaprayātam (भुजङ्गप्रयातम्).

Anuṣṭup Chhand (अनुष्टुप् छन्द)

For this game, we shall be using the Anushtup/Anushtubh meter, which is short, simple, and most widely used.
The following is a shloka composed in Anushtup and instructing its construction -


  श्लोके षष्ठं गुरुज्ञेयम्
   ऽ ऽ ऽऽ । ऽऽ ऽ
  सर्वत्र लघुपञ्चमम्।
   ऽऽ। ।। ऽ। ऽ
  द्विचतुष्पादयोः ह्रस्वम्
   ।। ऽऽ ।ऽ  ऽ ऽ
  सप्तमं दीर्घमन्ययोः।।
   ऽ।ऽ  ऽ। ऽ। ऽ

Hence, there are four lines or padas (पद), each consisting of eight syllables.
Characteristics of Anushtubh according to the above shloka -
1. The sixth syllable in each pada must be long.
2. The fifth syllable in each pada must be short.
3. The seventh syllable must be short in the second and fourth padas, and long in the first and third ones.

Code-mixing

To make this game more fun, we give you the option of code-mixing; in fact, we encourage you to use your imagination! In case you are unable to find the right word in Sanskrit, you can use an English word, declining it according to Sanskrit grammar. But as mentioned before, you WON'T be judged for grammar.
E.g., अहं थिङ्कामि - aham thinkaami, that is, 'I am thinking'!
